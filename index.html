(un)professional experience

What I actually did

Not what the job description said. What I actually did, why it mattered, and where relevant — why I left.

Royal College of Psychiatrists
CESR Coordinator
2019 — 2022

When I started, nobody at the organisation cared about CESR. It had been left to rot for years — and it showed. I didn't fix it by being a wizard. I fixed it by caring about the humans involved.

I loved that job. I didn't always love the organisation, but I loved the work and the people it was for.

Why I left After 2.5 years and a pass rate transformation that hadn't been seen in a decade, I received what was framed as a pay rise. My salary moved from £29,897 to £30,000 — because a benchmarking exercise reset the band floor. New starters, including people I was actively training, joined at the same rate. I asked to be placed at the midpoint of the band. HR sat on my job description rewrite for four months without responding. I left in June.

"Money doesn't bring you job satisfaction" — true. But paying someone the same as a person who started last month after 2.5 years of turning a decade of failure around isn't a pay philosophy. It's a slap dressed as a pay rise.
University of the Arts London
Programme Administration Manager, Fashion
2022 — 2023

UAL had a complicated relationship with its administrative staff. The prevailing culture was one where administrators genuinely believed themselves inferior to the academics — they didn't have ideas, because they didn't think that was their place. Within my first week, more colleagues than I'd ever encountered told me they were "just a bit of a technophobe" — which in an administrative role is not a personality trait. It's a gap that someone has decided is acceptable.

I did what I do. Found the inefficiencies, learnt the technology needed to fix them, invested real time in the people who'd been overlooked, and wasn't afraid to say what I thought to the people above me. It turned out they quite liked it.

I came in knowing very little about fashion. I learnt about it properly — because you cannot contribute meaningfully to a conversation you haven't made the effort to understand.
